# Stochastic Recurrence Equations and ARCH Tail Behavior

## Summary

Stochastic recurrence equations explain how heavy tails can arise endogenously from multiplicative dynamics. This is the durable bridge from perpetuities to ARCH or GARCH-style volatility models.

## Core equations

The generic recurrence is
$$Y_t=A_t+B_tY_{t-1}.$$

Under
$$\E \ln |B|<0,$$
the recursion admits a stationary solution in distribution.

For ARCH(1),
$$X_t=\sqrt{\beta+\lambda X_{t-1}^2}\,Z_t,$$
so the squared process satisfies
$$X_t^2=\beta Z_t^2+\lambda Z_t^2 X_{t-1}^2.$$

The tail index is determined by the multiplicative factor:
$$\E\!\paren{(\lambda Z^2)^\kappa}=1 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \Prob(|X|>x)\sim c x^{-2\kappa}.$$

## Main logic

### 1. Perpetuities and ARCH share the same structural recursion

The mathematics of discounted or accumulated random cash flows and volatility recursions is the same stochastic recurrence skeleton.

### 2. Heavy tails need not come from heavy-tailed shocks

Gaussian innovations can still produce Pareto-type stationary tails if the multiplicative recursion is strong enough.

### 3. Tail thickness and extremal behavior are dynamic objects

Once the heavy tail is generated by the recursion, clustering and extremal dependence become properties of the dynamics, not just of the one-step innovation law.

## Failure modes

- assuming light-tailed innovations imply light-tailed stationary distributions
- reading ARCH only as a variance model and ignoring its tail implications
- using Gaussian tail intuition after fitting a multiplicative volatility recursion

## Quant relevance

This method matters for:

- volatility models with endogenous heavy tails
- deciding whether tail risk comes from shocks or dynamics
- connecting ARCH or GARCH models to EVT and extremal clustering
- understanding why moment existence can fail in fitted volatility models
