# Causality and Factor Investing A Primer

## Summary

This short paper argues that factor investing needs causal thinking rather than purely correlational econometrics. The extracted section structure shows a direct argument from the factor zoo and factor mirage toward professional best practices and the economic cost of causal neglect.

## Section-by-Section Ingest

- `Abstract`: positions causal reasoning as the missing discipline in much of factor research.
- `1. Introduction`: frames the problem of treating factor evidence as sufficient without intervention-aware reasoning.
- `2. From the "Factor Zoo" to the "Factor Mirage"`: argues that proliferating reported factors can conceal weak causal grounding.
- `3. Where the Canon Fails: Econometrics without Causality`: critiques standard empirical practice when causal structure is left implicit.
- `4. Example: Colliders among Barra Factors`: gives a concrete example of how structural misunderstanding can distort factor interpretation.
- `5. Best Practices for Professionals and Asset Owners`: translates the critique into research and governance guidance.
- `6. The Economic Cost of Causal Neglect`: argues that ignoring causal structure creates real economic losses, not merely academic errors.
- `7. Conclusion`: closes by elevating causal discipline from optional refinement to professional necessity.

## Key Claims

- Factor research can look statistically rich while remaining structurally weak.
- Econometrics without causal structure can mislead portfolio and investment decisions.
- Professional factor investing needs explicit attention to confounding, colliders, and intervention logic.

## Methods and Data

- causal reasoning for factor models
- structural critique of factor research
- collider examples in risk-factor settings

## Why It Matters Here

This note is especially important for the factor and strategy side of the vault because it pushes the knowledge base away from purely associational alpha thinking.

## Reflection

The deepest lesson here is that a quant knowledge base cannot be satisfied with "it backtested." If the vault is going to support professional research, it also has to organize what is causal, what is descriptive, and what is merely predictive.

## Caveats

- This is a short conceptual primer, not a full causal-methods textbook.
- It should be paired with econometrics and design-based sources before making strong causal claims.
