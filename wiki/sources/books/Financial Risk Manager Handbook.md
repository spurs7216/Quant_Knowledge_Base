# Financial Risk Manager Handbook

## Summary

This handbook is a broad survey of quantitative analysis, capital markets, market risk, credit risk, operational and integrated risk, legal/accounting/tax risk, and regulation. It is less unified than a single-theory textbook, but it is valuable as a domain map of professional risk practice.

## Chapter-by-Chapter Ingest

- `Chapter 1. Bond Fundamentals`: starts with fixed-income pricing basics and sensitivity language.
- `Chapter 2. Fundamentals of Probability`: introduces the probability base needed for later risk measurement.
- `Chapter 3. Fundamentals of Statistics`: adds statistical estimation and variability concepts.
- `Chapter 4. Monte Carlo Methods`: brings simulation into valuation and risk estimation.
- `Chapter 5. Introduction to Derivatives`: surveys derivative claims and pricing intuition.
- `Chapter 6. Options`: treats option structures and sensitivities in more detail.
- `Chapter 7. Fixed-Income Securities`: studies bond-market instruments and term-structure objects.
- `Chapter 8. Fixed-Income Derivatives`: extends derivative treatment into rates markets.
- `Chapter 9. Equity Markets`: surveys market structure and instruments on the equity side.
- `Chapter 10. Currencies and Commodities Markets`: broadens the market coverage beyond stocks and bonds.
- `Chapter 11. Introduction to Market Risk Measurement`: frames VaR-style market-risk measurement.
- `Chapter 12. Identification of Risk Factors`: studies how portfolios inherit risk from underlying drivers.
- `Chapter 13. Sources of Risk`: categorizes and decomposes major market-risk exposures.
- `Chapter 14. Hedging Linear Risk`: handles first-order risk management.
- `Chapter 15. Nonlinear Risk: Options`: addresses convex exposures and nonlinear payoff risk.
- `Chapter 16. Modeling Risk Factors`: turns qualitative drivers into quantitative risk models.
- `Chapter 17. VaR Methods`: surveys the main approaches to value-at-risk.
- `Chapter 18. Introduction to Credit Risk`: begins the credit-risk layer.
- `Chapter 19. Measuring Actuarial Default Risk`: treats default through actuarial / transition-style views.
- `Chapter 20. Measuring Default Risk from Market Prices`: infers default risk from traded prices and spreads.
- `Chapter 21. Credit Exposure`: studies exposure measurement for counterparties and positions.
- `Chapter 22. Credit Derivatives`: covers instruments designed to transfer credit risk.
- `Chapter 23. Managing Credit Risk`: treats portfolio and process-level credit-risk control.
- `Chapter 24. Operational Risk`: broadens risk beyond market and credit losses.
- `Chapter 25. Risk Capital and RAROC`: links risk to capital allocation and performance measurement.
- `Chapter 26. Best Practices Reports`: surveys governance and practice standards.
- `Chapter 27. Firmwide Risk Management`: integrates risk types at the enterprise level.
- `Chapter 28. Legal Issues`: addresses contractual and legal exposure.
- `Chapter 29. Accounting and Tax Issues`: connects risk management with accounting and tax treatment.
- `Chapter 30. Regulation of Financial Institutions`: covers the regulatory context around risk.
- `Chapter 31. The Basel Accord`: studies capital regulation at the framework level.
- `Chapter 32. The Basel Market Risk Charges`: focuses on regulatory treatment of market-risk capital.

## Key Claims

- Risk management is not one model family; it is an integrated stack spanning instruments, exposures, governance, and regulation.
- Market, credit, operational, legal, and capital questions interact and cannot be managed in isolation.
- Quantitative risk metrics are only useful when tied to process and governance.

## Methods and Data

- fixed-income analytics and derivatives
- Monte Carlo and VaR
- market and credit-risk factor modeling
- exposure measurement and credit derivatives
- RAROC, capital allocation, and regulatory frameworks

## Why It Matters Here

This handbook broadens the vault's risk layer beyond alpha research and pricing theory. It is especially useful for keeping enterprise risk, capital, and regulation visible alongside narrower model notes.

## Reflection

The handbook is less elegant than a focused theory text, but it captures a truth quants often neglect: real financial risk work sits at the boundary of mathematics, institutions, accounting, and regulation.

## Caveats

- The material reflects an early-2000s FRM framing and should be read historically, especially around regulation and post-crisis practice.
- The book is broad and survey-like; it is stronger as a map than as the final word on any single risk topic.
