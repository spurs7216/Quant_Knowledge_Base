## Role of the chapter

This chapter is the first full answer to the incomplete-market question "which martingale measure should we use?"

## Core mathematical objects

- Esscher kernel $\psi^E$
- associated Girsanov kernel $\phi^E$
- minimal martingale measure $Q^M$
- martingale equation

## Structural map of the chapter

- define standard and generalized Esscher transforms
- solve the martingale equation in general and Markov-factor models
- define the minimal martingale measure
- compare the resulting measure choices economically

## Theorem and derivation spine

### Esscher selects one martingale measure by solving the martingale equation

The rendered theorem page gives the core formula:
$$
\psi_t^E=(\sigma_t\sigma_t^\ast)^{-1}(r_t-\mu_t),
$$
with Girsanov kernel
$$
\phi_t^E=\sigma_t^\ast(\sigma_t\sigma_t^\ast)^{-1}(r_t-\mu_t).
$$

That is the distinguished martingale measure generated by the Esscher transform in the model class considered.

### Minimal martingale measure keeps nonpriced noise untouched

The chapter's second major idea is that the minimal martingale measure neutralizes priced risk while disturbing orthogonal nonpriced martingale components as little as possible.

## Failure modes and misuse points

- treating the Esscher measure as the unique economically correct choice in all incomplete models
- forgetting that different measure-selection criteria generally imply different prices
- using the minimal martingale measure without asking whether its economic interpretation fits the application

## Quant research relevance

This chapter matters in incomplete-market pricing, especially when one needs a disciplined but tractable measure choice in factor models.
