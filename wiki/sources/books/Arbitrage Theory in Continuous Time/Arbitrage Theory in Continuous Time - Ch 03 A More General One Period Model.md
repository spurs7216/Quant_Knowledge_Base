## Role of the chapter

This chapter is the finite-dimensional algebraic core of the whole book.

The binomial model is special because everything is two-dimensional and explicit. Chapter 3 removes that special structure and shows that no-arbitrage, martingale measures, completeness, and discount factors are linear-duality results.

## Core mathematical objects

- normalized price vector $z_0$
- terminal normalized payoffs $z(\omega_j)$
- strictly positive state-price weights $q_j$
- stochastic discount factor $m_j$
- attainable-claim span and uniqueness of pricing functionals

## Structural map of the chapter

- normalize prices under a numeraire
- use convex duality to characterize absence of arbitrage
- interpret the dual object as a martingale measure or state-price vector
- connect uniqueness of the dual object with completeness
- rewrite pricing with stochastic discount factors

## Theorem and derivation spine

### Farkas' Lemma is the engine behind one-period no-arbitrage

The rendered theorem page states the finite-dimensional dichotomy:

- either a target payoff vector lies in the nonnegative cone generated by the payoff columns
- or there exists a separating hyperplane producing a strict negative value on the target and nonnegative values on the cone

That is exactly the algebraic shape of "replicable versus arbitrage-separating portfolio."

### Absence of arbitrage is equivalent to existence of strictly positive pricing weights

After normalization, arbitrage freedom means there exists a strictly positive vector of state prices or martingale probabilities that prices every traded asset consistently.

In one-period notation, the pricing relation takes the form
$$
z_0=\sum_{j=1}^M q_j z(\omega_j),
$$
with $q_j>0$ and $\sum_j q_j=1$ after normalization.

### Completeness is uniqueness of the pricing functional

The market is complete if every claim is in the span of traded payoffs. In the dual picture, that means the martingale measure or state-price vector is unique.

This is the first clean appearance of a theme that later survives unchanged:

- no-arbitrage corresponds to existence
- completeness corresponds to uniqueness

### Stochastic discount factors are dual-price objects

The chapter's SDF treatment shows that pricing can be written either through state prices, martingale probabilities, or discount factors. This becomes important later when the book moves from arbitrage-only pricing to equilibrium pricing.

## Failure modes and misuse points

- thinking of martingale measures as purely continuous-time objects
- forgetting that completeness is dual to uniqueness of prices, not only to hedge rhetoric
- treating stochastic discount factors as an equilibrium-only concept
