# Probability Theory The Logic of Science - Ch 15 Paradoxes of Probability Theory

## Ingest Scope and Depth

## Role of the chapter

This chapter is a warning against treating formal paradoxes as proof that probability is broken. Jaynes' line is that most paradoxes are generated by silently changing the state of information, not by defects in the probability rules.

## Core ideas

- a probability statement is always conditional on information
- paradoxes often arise from mixing incompatible conditioning sets
- "uninformative" formulations are usually not invariant across parameterizations
- optional stopping, marginalization, or nonconglomerability disputes often hide information-loss problems

## What matters later

The chapter trains the reader to inspect the information state before blaming the calculus. That discipline matters in model comparison, prior selection, and all later Bayesian/frequentist clashes in the book.

## Failure modes and misuse points

- comparing answers that were conditioned on different background information
- treating a reparameterization as innocuous when it changes the implied prior
- using paradoxes rhetorically instead of resolving the underlying conditioning error

## Quant research relevance

Many backtest "paradoxes" are really conditioning failures: sample reuse, implicit look-ahead, post-selection inference, or unstable reference classes. This chapter is an early inoculation against those errors.
