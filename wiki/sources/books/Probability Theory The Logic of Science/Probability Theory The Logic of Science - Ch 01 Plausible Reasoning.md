# Probability Theory The Logic of Science - Ch 01 Plausible Reasoning

## Ingest Scope and Depth

- Ingest stage: `deep`
- Pass 1: full chapter scan completed.
- Pass 2: deepened the foundational distinction between deductive certainty and plausible reasoning.

## Role of the chapter

This chapter states the book's governing claim: most real inference is not deductive. We act under incomplete information, so the right logic must quantify graded plausibility rather than only certainty.

## Core objects

- deductive reasoning
- plausible reasoning
- background information `I`
- degrees of plausibility

## Theorem and derivation spine

The chapter is pre-formal, but its load-bearing move is conceptual. If plausibilities are to be manipulated consistently, then they must admit numerical representation. That is the opening step that later makes Cox-style rule derivation possible.

The durable inference picture is:

$$\text{logic under certainty} \;\longrightarrow\; \text{logic under incomplete information}.$$

## Why this matters later

Without this move, Bayesian updating looks like a special trick. With it, Bayesian probability becomes the continuation of logic into uncertain settings.

## Failure modes and misuse points

- treating probability as only long-run frequency
- confusing plausible inference with arbitrary subjectivity
- assuming deductive standards are the only legitimate standards of reasoning

## Quant research relevance

Quant work constantly requires decisions before certainty exists. This chapter is the philosophical and logical justification for why posterior beliefs, not slogans about objectivity, should guide research updates.
