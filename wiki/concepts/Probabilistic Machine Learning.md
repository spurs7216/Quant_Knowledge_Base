# Probabilistic Machine Learning

## Summary

Probabilistic machine learning treats learning as modeling uncertainty, latent structure, and decision-relevant quantities rather than only fitting a prediction surface. In this vault, it is the main bridge between econometrics, state-space methods, graphical models, Bayesian inference, and modern representation learning.

## Source Synthesis

- [[Bishop Pattern Recognition and Machine Learning]] provides the classical probabilistic foundation through latent variables, graphical models, kernels, and approximate inference.
- [[Murphy Probabilistic Machine Learning An Introduction]] broadens the core toolkit into GLMs, Gaussian processes, HMMs, state-space models, variational methods, and Monte Carlo inference.
- [[Murphy Probabilistic Machine Learning]] extends the framework into filtering and smoothing, deep generative models, representation learning, decision making, reinforcement learning, and causality.
- [[Bayesian Reasoning and Machine Learning]] keeps the structural emphasis on graphical models, hidden variables, switching dynamics, and Bayesian model comparison.

## What It Does

- models uncertainty explicitly
- separates observed variables from latent states
- supports sequential inference and hidden-state reasoning
- makes prediction, inference, and decision making part of one pipeline

## Why It Matters for Quant Research

- financial data are noisy, incomplete, and often generated by hidden regimes
- latent-state models are central for volatility, trend, and market-state inference
- posterior uncertainty is often more useful than point forecasts alone
- causal and decision layers matter once the research moves from prediction to capital allocation

## Typical Model Families

- Bayesian linear and generalized linear models
- Gaussian processes
- graphical models
- HMMs and state-space models
- mixture and latent-factor models
- variational and Monte Carlo inference frameworks

## Failure Modes

- treating hidden-state models as if the latent state were directly observed
- ignoring model misspecification while reporting overly sharp uncertainty estimates
- using powerful approximate inference without checking whether the approximation changes the economic conclusion
- confusing predictive success with structural understanding
